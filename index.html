About Me

I am a sign language linguist working on Kata Kolok, a sign language that emerged spontaneously in a village community in Bali. Currently, I am a postdoc at the ERC-funded SignMorph Project at University of Birmingham, headed by Adam Schembri .

My research interests cover variation, language documentation and language acquisition. Specifically, I am interested in the emergence of Kata Kolok and how variation patterns across different signers from the community. I also study how deaf children learn Kata Kolok in a linguistically rich environment.

I hold a Bachelor degree in "Sign Languages" from Hamburg University, Germany, and a research Master degree in "Linguistics", specialised in Sign Language Linguistics, from University of Amsterdam, The Netherlands. I obtained my PhD in March 2022 from Radboud University Nijmegen, The Netherlands.

I am also a yoga teacher! Find some more information in the section Yoga!

Research

Most of my research focuses on Kata Kolok, a sign language used in a rural enclave in Bali, Indonesia. My work so far has been a combination of language documentation, research into different aspects of variation on several levels of linguistic description and research into language acquisition.

Variation

I have explored variation in the form of signs (or phonology) and the lexicon of Kata Kolok.

In co-authored work with Katie Mudd, we have found that whether Kata Kolok signers are hearing or deaf influences their choice of signs in a picture elicitation task. We used a lexical distance measure to analyse people's responses. Building up on this work, we are currently working on a cross-linguistic comparison, comparing our data from Kata Kolok and data from British Sign Language and Israeli Sign Language. This is particularly exciting because the literature suggests that smaller sign languages, like Kata Kolok, show more lexical variation than bigger sign languages. In addition, we are looking into lexical variation among Balinese Homesigners within the ELISA project, headed by Connie de Vos.



Acquisition of Kata Kolok

Some of my favourite projects are focused on how deaf children acquire Kata Kolok - I have used observational and experimental data.

In a study based on naturalistic data, I have analysed many hours of longitudinal recordings of four deaf children for child modifications of signs, i.e. signs that deviate from the adult target. The focus children grow up with many of their immediate family members being deaf, but also many hearing relatives who can sign. I analysed these signs using a feature-coding used for adult signers as well. I found that the data includes most modifications in handshape but that location features are often changed as well.

In addition, I have designed and conducted a low-tech version of an familiarisation paradigm with signing and non-signing children in the community. In this study, I have animated a monkey to sign Kata Kolok sign and we recorded signing children (hearing and deaf) and non-signing children while watching the monkey sign correct Kata Kolok signs or mispronounced Kata Kolok signs. I found that the traditional analyses do not reveal any group differences but additional behavioural analyses may point to differences between groups. This project is crucial to kickstarting experimental acquisition work in the field - an endeavour that is not often done due to the many challenges met.


Name signs in Kata Kolok and Sign Language of the Netherlands

In this project, I looked at the form of name signs, i.e. signs attributed to people, in Kata Kolok and Sign Language of the Netherlands.

Name signs in Kata Kolok are descriptive. In NGT, they may incorporate fingerspelling. In both languages, name signs tend to be one-handed and located around the head. Only KK name signs comprise many basic handshapes and often make contact with the body. Very different is what the mouth does: in NGT, signers always imitate the spoken name; in Kata Kolok, signers never imitate the name but often use nonmanuals. My favourite finding is that Kata Kolok has nonmanual name signs!



Negation in Kata Kolok

For my MA thesis, I studied how negation is expressed in Kata Kolok, using spontaneous data from the Kata Kolok Corpus.

I investigated spontaneous dyadic conversations of six deaf Kata Kolok signers from three subsequent generations as to the form of the negator they use and the position of the negator. My data suggests that, against what has been previously reported, Kata Kolok signers use a manual sign and a negative side-to-side headshake to negate propositions. Both markers probably have a origin in co-speech gesture. Non-existentials are marked with tongue protrusion, which is not found in co-speech gesture, but maybe there is a link to the daemon Rangda from Balinese mythology.

Current Projects

At the moment, I am involved in several different projects which is very exciting!

Morphological Complexity
In this project, we are looking into the notion of "linguistic complexity" and problematises how this has been used in the literature - this work is in collaboration with Rehana Omardeen , Felicia Bisnath , and SignMorphers Marah Jaraisy and Adam Schembri .

Lexical Variation
In this project, we are exploring lexical variation a bit more. This work builds up on a continuous collaboration with Katie Mudd . In one of the two projects, we are working on a cross-linguistic comparison of lexical variation in Kata Kolok, British Sign Language and Israeli Sign Language. This work is in collaboration with Adam Schembri and Rose Stamp.
I am also involved in an exciting project exploring lexical variation in Balinese Homesigners and comparing it to our published work on lexical variation in Kata Kolok - this work is headed by Connie de Vos and Satyawati of the ELISA project and Katie Mudd !

Acquisition of Kata Kolok phonology
I am still wrapping up a project of an experimental study in the field with signing and non-signing children. This work was done with Connie de Vos, Paula Fikkert and Onno Crasborn. Fingers crossed this paper will find a good home!

Other Initiated Repair
Another side project of mine goes back to a project done in my Masters - a comparison of Other Initiated Repair and Backchanel Responses in British Sign Language and spoken English based on video corpora. This work is in collaboration with Lierin Wael, Rehana Omardeen and Mark Dingemanse .

Yoga

I am really excited about sharing the time and the practice with others and love seeing them enjoy their time on their mat! I'd describe my style as energetic and dynamic but not too intense.

Ashtanga

In 2019, I completed a Yoga Teacher Training (200hrs) with Annemieke van der Zouwen, Joeri Roelandt, and Helena Peirtsegaele at Ashtanga Yoga Academy. Ashtanga is a dymanic form of yoga and the practice is designed around several series of Asanas. Ever since I got my certification, I have been teaching a weekly mixed levels Ashtanga-inspired class online.
The class currently takes place on Sunday evening, 19:30-20:30 CET. Get in touch if you are interested in joining!


Pre- and post-natal Yoga

In 2021, I completed a training to certify as AYA pre- and post-natal yoga teacher (100hrs) at Unit. Shortly after, I got pregnant myself and used all knowledge during my own pregnancy, birth and postnatal practice. I have also taught some individual classes.
